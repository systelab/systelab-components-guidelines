Dialog

Dialogs inform users about a task and can contain decisive information that requires immediate attention.

Overview

A dialog is a modal window that appears in front of app content to provide critical information or ask for a decision.

Dialogs disable all app functionality when they appear, and remain on screen until confirmed, dismissed, or a required action has been taken.

Dialogs are purposefully interruptive, so they should be used sparingly. A less disruptive alternative is to use a menu, which provides options without interrupting a user’s experience.

When to use a modal:

  • To grab user’s attention for an important message.
  • To show additional information without losing the context of the parent page.
  • To break down a complex workflow into a series of less complicated steps.
When not to use a modal:
  • For nonessential information that is not related to the current workflow.
  • For complex workflows that require additional information not contained within the modal.
  • Avoid modal over modal. Context becomes confusing when modals are stacked.

Styles

Title

The title summarizes the purpose of the modal. In a confirmation modal, use a question in your title and make sure the action is clearly called out in the buttons.

Content

The content may differ between different types of modals. Be concise and provide a clear explanation of the task or information a user should focus on.

Buttons

Buttons should include clear and direct action for the user to take. Buttons are right-aligned based on the Z Pattern.

Stacking dialogs

Dialogs shouldn’t launch other dialogs.

Stacking dialogs makes it hard to dismiss them and confuses the user on their levels of importance.

If you see a need to stack dialogs, you should:
  • Seek alternatives to the second modal such as inline expansion within the first one.
  • Seek alternatives to the use of dialogs in that scenario and explore alternatives such as inline task completion.

Anatomy

Container and scrim

Dialog containers appear above other screen elements and hold the dialog’s header, content and footer. To focus attention on the dialog, surfaces behind the container are scrimmed with a temporary overlay to make them less prominent.

Header

Dialog containers appear above other screen elements and hold the dialog’s header, content and footer. To focus attention on the dialog, surfaces behind the container are scrimmed with a temporary overlay to make them less prominent.

Footer

Dialog actions are most often represented as buttons and allow users to confirm, dismiss, or acknowledge something.

To focus attention on the dialog, surfaces behind the container are scrimmed with a temporary overlay to make them less prominent.

Confirming actions are aligned to the ending side of the screen with dismissive actions on the leading side of the confirming action.

Other actions to access further functionality can be added and are aligned to the starting side of the screen.

Navbar (Optional)

Dialog actions are most often represented as buttons and allow users to confirm, dismiss, or acknowledge something.

To focus attention on the dialog, surfaces behind the container are scrimmed with a temporary overlay to make them less prominent.

Confirming actions are aligned to the ending side of the screen with dismissive actions on the leading side of the confirming action.

Other actions to access further functionality can be added and are aligned to the starting side of the screen.

Avoid placing any other element in the footer rather than buttons.

Sizes

Small

Consider using small dialogs for the following scenarios: alerting the user, confirmation dialogs and task-oriented workflows. Max width 440px.

Medium (Vertical form)

Consider using small dialogs for the following scenarios: alerting the user, confirmation dialogs and task-oriented workflows. Max width 440px.

Large

Consider using small dialogs for the following scenarios: alerting the user, confirmation dialogs, and task-oriented workflows. Max width 880 px.

Full screen

When there is a lot of information and a lot of screen space is needed.

Responsive layout

When scaling layouts for larger screens, adjust a dialog’s visual presentation by setting minimum and maximum values for margins, padding, and container dimensions.

Dialogs should maintain a minimum 48dp distance from the leading and trailing edges of a screen. On small screens, dialogs scale vertically until reaching 48dp from the screen’s top and bottom edge. On larger displays, dialogs can increase in size to a maximum width of 560dp and maintain a distance of 56dp from the edges of a screen. When a dialog reaches the maximum width, it expands or contracts vertically to support content, up to a maximum height of 560dp.

When using systelab-dialog, use widthRelative and heightRelative to specify size in terms like '75%' or '40vh'. It is suggested also to specify min and max width and height.

Position

Dialogs retain focus until dismissed or an action has been taken, such as choosing a setting. They shouldn’t be obscured by other elements or appear partially on screen, with the exception of full-screen dialogs.

Scrolling

Most dialog content should avoid scrolling. Even when scrolling is required, the dialog title is pinned at the top, with buttons pinned at the bottom. This ensures selected content remains visible alongside the title and buttons, even upon scroll.

Dialogs don’t scroll with elements outside of the dialog, such as the background.

Dismissing dialogs

Dialogs may be dismissed by:

  • Tapping a "close" icon button put on header on the right side
  • Tapping a "cancel" button, if one is shown
  • Pressing the keyboard Escape key
If the user’s ability to dismiss a dialog is disabled, the user must choose a dialog action to proceed.
